The Lancashire Family of Michigan (Late 1800s – Early 1900s)

A documented family-history narrative tracing the Lancashire family’s migration from England, early enterprises in Michigan, later prominence in Detroit retail, and connections to the Connor and MacDonald families.

Early Settlement in Michigan: Lumber and General Store

The Lancashire family emigrated from England in the late 19th century and settled in Michigan during the post–Civil War lumber boom. By the early 1870s they were established in Osceola County, where William H. Lancashire and his son Lawrence H. Lancashire operated a sawmill in Evart [1]. The family also operated one of Evart’s early general stores, serving lumbermen and settlers [2].

The family matriarch, Hannah W. (Winterton) Lancashire (1817–1898), lived in Evart and is buried there, marking the close of the family’s pioneer generation [3].

Rise in Detroit: Lawrence Lancashire and Mabley’s Department Store

Lawrence Henry Lancashire (1851–1895) moved to Detroit in the late 1870s and joined C.R. Mabley & Co., Detroit’s first major department store [4].

Following the death of founder Christopher R. Mabley in 1885, the business reorganized under senior managers. By 1886, Lawrence Lancashire served as Vice President of Mabley & Co., a role documented in contemporary Detroit press accounts [5].

Under this leadership, Mabley’s remained one of Michigan’s largest retail establishments, expanding along Woodward Avenue and shaping Detroit’s commercial core [6].

Marriage into the Connor Family

In 1879, Lawrence Lancashire married Mary Josephine Connor of Grosse Pointe [7]. She was a descendant of the Connor (Conner) family, whose patriarch Henry Conner owned extensive land along Detroit’s east side.

Conner’s Creek—and later Conner Avenue—were named for Henry Conner, whose holdings shaped early Detroit geography [8].

Lawrence and Mary had four children: Forrest Henry, Marie L., Herbert Winterton, and Lucy Hazel Lancashire [9].

The family later resided in an affluent home on Lone Pine Road in Birmingham, Michigan, reflecting their social standing among Detroit-area business families.

Family Branches

California

Forrest Henry Lancashire eventually relocated to California, where he lived until his death in 1949 [10].

Toledo, Ohio

Herbert Winterton Lancashire settled in Toledo after marrying Martha Ireton McDonald in 1915 [11]. Family and newspaper accounts associate Herbert with ownership of the Buckeye Brewery and an early Ford dealership in Toledo [12].

Michigan

Marie L. Lancashire Keenan remained in Michigan, while Lucy Hazel Lancashire Bird lived until 1968, representing the last generation born into Detroit’s 19th-century Lancashire household [13].

The MacDonald Connection and Glencoe Hills

Herbert’s wife, Martha Ireton McDonald, was the only daughter of James Henry McDonald (1853–1934), a prominent Detroit lawyer and University of Michigan alumnus [14].

In the 1920s, James McDonald built the estate known as Glencoe Hills near Ann Arbor, a large country property later documented by the Ann Arbor Observer [15].
